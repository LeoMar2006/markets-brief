## 3. What's Moving Markets

### 1. The Iran Deal — THE Weekend Binary

President Trump wrote on Truth Social that the US-Iran nuclear and military deal is "scheduled to get signed tomorrow" (i.e., Sunday June 14), with the Strait of Hormuz reopening immediately upon signing. The deal as understood includes: Strait reopening; dismantlement of Iran's nuclear enrichment programme; removal of enriched uranium stockpile (to be destroyed/exported); an international inspection regime; and phased lifting of energy sanctions. Iran's Foreign Ministry has publicly disputed the Sunday timeline, saying signing "will not be tomorrow," but has not denied that an agreement is close. UK PM Starmer called Trump on Saturday to express support; Pakistani PM Sharif said a deal was "likely in the next 24 hours."

Markets are in an awkward position: oil has already moved sharply to price in a deal ($86.5 vs $105+ a few weeks ago), equities have rallied on the disinflation narrative, and the DXY has softened on reduced risk premium. If the deal prints Monday, the additional move in oil may be capped (much is priced), though a sustainable break below $80 becomes plausible as Iranian supply normalization progresses. If Iran's hardliners block signing or technical issues delay it, expect a sharp reversal in oil (+$5–10) and a risk-off tone across equities.

### 2. Oil — Priced for Peace, Not Yet for Policy

Even if the deal signs, the physical oil market takes time to normalize. The Strait of Hormuz can technically reopen within days, but Iranian sanction relief takes negotiation and US Congressional involvement. Iran's official oil production capacity is ~3–3.5m b/d, but much has been suppressed under sanctions. Restoration of even 1m b/d of net incremental supply would be meaningful against a global demand base of ~103m b/d. OPEC+ dynamics also shift — Saudi Arabia's fiscal breakeven is around $90/bbl and may resist further price drops. The downside for Brent is real but may be more gradual than the headline suggests.

### 3. FOMC Wednesday — Warsh's First Dot Plot

The June 16–17 FOMC is Warsh's first meeting with a full dot plot and Summary of Economic Projections. The rate decision itself is irrelevant (hold at 3.5–3.75% is nearly certain). What markets are watching: (a) does the median dot for 2026 shift back toward 4% or higher, signalling a potential hike? (b) does Warsh use the press conference to formally retire the easing bias that existed under Powell? (c) how does the SEP handle the energy/inflation interaction given that oil has fallen 20%? US CPI at 4.2% YoY is 220bps above target, which alone would justify Warsh caution. The 2yr UST at 4.09% would need to re-rate toward 4.25–4.40% if the dot plot shows a median hike in 2026.

### 4. ECB's Stagflation Hike

Last Thursday's ECB move — 25bps to 2.25%, first hike since 2023 — crystallized the stagflation bind. Eurozone CPI accelerated to 3.2% headline in May (core 2.5%), driven entirely by the Iran war's energy pass-through. But EZ GDP contracted 0.2% in Q1 2026, and the ECB now forecasts 2026 growth of just 0.8%. Lagarde framed the hike as "pre-emptive against entrenched inflation" while acknowledging deteriorating growth. The Bund at 3.05% is near recent highs. A second ECB hike is on the table for Q3 — but Iran deal-driven oil disinflation could make it unnecessary, and the ECB is deeply reluctant to break already-fragile EZ economies.

### 5. Bank of England on a Knife Edge

The April MPC vote was 8-1 — one Hawk (believed to be Jonathan Haskel's replacement) voting for a 25bps hike to 4%. This was the most hawkish split since early 2023. The BoE faces genuine inflation uncertainty: UK services CPI has been sticky above 5%, and energy cost pass-through is adding to headline. Wednesday's UK CPI print is critical — if May CPI comes in above 3.5% headline or if services CPI accelerates, a second hawk may emerge for Thursday's decision. GBP/USD at 1.340 has BoE hawkishness partially priced; an 8-1 hold with a more hawkish statement could push it toward 1.355.

### 6. Gold Holding Near $4,224 — Central Bank Bid Dominates

Gold at $4,224 is puzzling to some: geopolitical risk is fading (Iran deal), but gold is holding. The explanation is structural: central banks (primarily China, India, Turkey, and various EM sovereigns) have been running persistent buying programmes as dollar reserve diversification. DXY at 99.8 is a tailwind. Real US rates — which are the gold market's traditional kryptonite — are not rising sharply because the Fed is on hold and inflation at 4.2% keeps real rates relatively low. The one clear negative catalyst for gold would be a Warsh press conference that signals aggressive hike-readiness, which would spike real rates and strengthen the dollar.

### 7. Copper — $14,000/t and a Supply Deficit

Copper's rally is structural, not just cyclical. Goldman Sachs estimates a 2026 supply deficit of 330,000 tonnes against a backdrop of deepening demand from AI data centres (which need ~12x more copper per MW than conventional builds), EV batteries, and grid infrastructure upgrades. Chilean state copper commission Cochilco raised its 2026 price forecast to ~$5.55/lb. LME copper inventories are tight. Chinese smelters are reportedly buying pyrite as a substitute feedstock due to copper concentrate shortages, suggesting genuine tightness at the smelting stage. This is not a story that gets derailed by an Iran deal.

### 8. Japan Riding the Weak Yen / AI Wave

The Nikkei's +2.90% Friday session brings it to 66,078 — near its 2026 highs. The dual engines are: (1) USD/JPY at ~158 inflating yen-based export earnings and making Japan globally price-competitive; (2) global technology/AI enthusiasm lifting Japan's semiconductor equipment and factory automation sectors (Shin-Etsu Chemical, Advantest, Tokyo Electron). The BoJ is in an impossible position — raising rates risks a brutal carry trade unwind, while staying on hold risks further yen weakness toward 160+ where it faced intervention previously.
